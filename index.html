My Why

I've spent my career at the intersection of technical knowledge and teaching — a combination that's rarer than people think. Most CS professionals can't teach. Most educators can't go deep on the content. I can do both, and I've built programs, trained teachers, and designed curriculum to prove it. What drives me is the belief that learning systems — whether a course, a department, or a training program — can be intentionally designed to actually work. That's what I do.

What the work looks like

Curriculum Design

Building a CS Pathway from Scratch

When San Diego Unified School District needed to stand up a coherent K–12 CS pathway, there was no existing curriculum, no trained teachers, and no clear scope and sequence. I led the design process end-to-end — mapping standards, developing courses, and building the teacher support structures to sustain it.

Outcome: A multi-year, standards-aligned CS pathway now serves hundreds of students annually, with a teacher training model that didn't require hiring new staff.

Program Leadership

Turning Around a Struggling Department

I inherited a CS program with inconsistent instruction, low enrollment, and no clear identity. Over two years, I redesigned the course offerings, rebuilt the instructional culture, and repositioned the program to serve a broader range of students — not just those already planning to major in CS.

Outcome: Enrollment grew significantly. More importantly, the program became one students wanted to be in — not one they stumbled into.

Workforce & Adult Learning

Training Teachers to Teach CS

Most professional development for CS teachers is either too technical or too surface-level. I designed and facilitated a training model that meets teachers where they are — building both content knowledge and pedagogical confidence simultaneously.

Outcome: Participants consistently reported feeling ready to teach the content, not just aware of it — a meaningful difference for adult learners with high stakes.

Teaching & Leadership Philosophy

How I think about learning

Learning is a design problem.

Good teaching isn't improvised — it's engineered. Every course, unit, and lesson I build starts with a clear picture of what learners should be able to do, then works backwards to design the experiences that get them there. This applies equally to a high school CS classroom and a corporate onboarding program.

Context shapes everything.

I don't believe in generic curriculum or one-size-fits-all training. Effective learning design requires understanding who is in the room, what they already know, what they're afraid of, and what success actually looks like for them. My job is to make that visible — and then design for it.

Leaders build systems, not dependencies.

My goal as a program leader is always to build something that outlasts me. That means investing in teachers, documenting decisions, and creating structures that hold even when key people leave. Sustainable programs are designed, not inherited.
